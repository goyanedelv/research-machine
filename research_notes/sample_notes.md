# Research Notes: Impact of Artificial Intelligence on the Labor Market

## Introduction Context
The advent of Artificial Intelligence (AI)—and particularly Generative AI (GenAI)—represents a fundamental shift in technological disruption of the labor market. Unlike earlier automation waves (e.g., robotics and basic computing) which primarily affected routine, manual, and basic administrative tasks, recent AI developments extend into cognitive, creative, and non-routine problem-solving domains. This broadens the scope of "AI exposure" significantly, moving up the skill ladder to affect knowledge workers, management, and specialized professionals. The rapid pace of this technological evolution is currently outpacing the ability of organizations to restructure workflows and measure precise long-term labor impacts.

## Key Findings
- **Muted Aggregate Impacts to Date:** Despite widespread discussion, empirical studies examining the initial waves of AI adoption (often up to 2023) show muted aggregate impacts on overall employment and wages. Many early findings suggest that AI is currently too small relative to the scale of global labor markets to cause first-order macro employment disruptions outside of AI-specific hiring itself.
- **Displacement vs. Augmentation:** The net impact of AI on labor depends on the balance between three forces: the displacement effect (AI substituting human labor), the productivity/scale effect (AI making firms more efficient, thus demanding more labor), and the reinstatement effect (AI creating new tasks for humans). Several studies indicate that for many high-skill roles, the productivity and augmentation effects currently offset the displacement effects, potentially leading to net positive medium-term outcomes in some sectors.
- **Heterogeneous Exposure and Vulnerability:** High AI exposure affects a significant portion of the workforce (e.g., up to a third in the US). However, vulnerability varies based on "adaptive capacity." For example, clerical and administrative support roles often face high exposure but possess lower adaptive capacity, making them more susceptible to negative displacement. Conversely, early-career workers in cognitive fields are experiencing shifts in hiring and entry-level task structures.
- **Impact on Inequality:** Early OECD evidence suggests that AI might actually reduce wage inequality within certain occupations by raising the performance floor—acting as an equalizer that boosts the productivity of lower-performing workers more than high-performing ones. However, broader macroeconomic inequality remains a concern if AI benefits accrue primarily to capital owners or highly specialized tech hubs.

## Methodologies
- **Task-Based Framework:** Most economic research relies on the "task-based framework" (Autor, Levy, and Murnane 2003; Acemoglu and Restrepo 2011). This methodology conceptualizes jobs as bundles of tasks and evaluates which specific tasks within an occupation can be automated or augmented by AI.
- **AI Exposure Indexes:** Researchers frequently construct "AI Exposure" indices by mapping AI capabilities (e.g., from benchmarks or LLM evaluations) to occupational task descriptions found in databases like O*NET.
- **Microdata and Vacancy Analysis:** Studies utilize firm-level vacancy postings, online freelance labor market data, and large-scale demographic surveys (e.g., the US Current Population Survey or OECD surveys) to track hiring shifts, wage premiums, and unemployment risks across different demographics and sectors.
